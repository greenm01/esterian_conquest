== Introduction

Beyond the mapped frontiers of the old Esterian dominion lies a small galaxy of contested solar systems. The old masters are gone. Their stations are silent, their patrols vanished, and their subjects left with fleets, factories, and enough knowledge to build empires.

You rise as one of the new Star Masters. From a single world and a few small fleets, you must tax, build, scout, bargain, threaten, and strike before rival powers can do the same. Some systems will join your banner willingly. Others will require persuasion from orbit.

Each maintenance marks the passage of a year. In that span, fleets cross the dark between stars, colonies grow or starve, alliances turn cold, and wars are decided by distance, industry, mathematics, and will.

In profound respect and admiration to Bentley C. Griffith and his fellow pioneers, who between 1990 and 1992 forged the enduring legend of Esterian Conquest, and to the ancient dreamers, strategists, and storytellers whose timeless visions of galactic dominion still light the way among these stars.

== Quick Start

Your objective is simple: become Emperor by dominating rivals or eliminating every serious threat.

You begin with one planet at 100 production and four fleets: two carrying an ETAC and cruiser for colonization, two single-destroyer scouts.

Set a tax rate around 50-65% on your homeworld. Use revenue to build ships, armies, batteries, and starbases. Lower taxes on new colonies to accelerate growth.

Each round is one year. Orders are submitted during the year; maintenance resolves all empires simultaneously on an internal 52-week timeline. Reports use `Stardate WK/YYYY` (week/year).

=== Assets You Can Build

| Destroyer | 5 | S | 6 | 1 | 1 | Combat, scouting, defense
| Cruiser | 15 | M | 5 | 3 | 3 | Balanced combat
| Battleship | 45 | L | 4 | 9 | 10 | Heavy combat
| Scout | 15 | S | 6 | 0 | 1 | Reconnaissance
| Troop Transport | 5 | M | 5 | 0 | 1 | Deliver armies
| ETAC | 20 | L | 3 | 0 | 2 | Colonize raw planets (reusable)
| Ground Battery | 20 | L | - | 9 | 2 | Planetary defense
| Army | 2 | S | - | 1 | 1 | Surface defense and capture
| Starbase | 50 | L | 1 | 10 | 12 | Defense, production boost
|===

*   **AS**: Attack Strength (firepower)
*   **DS**: Defense Strength (hull/shields)

=== Fleet Missions (Summary)

Fleet missions fall into three categories:

*   *One-shot*: The fleet travels, performs the action, then reverts to Hold Position. You must issue new orders afterward.
*   *Persistent*: The mission remains armed until you replace it or game rules invalidate it.
*   *Hostile*: The fleet travels to the target world and waits. The assault executes on the *next* maintenance tick after the fleet is in orbit -- not immediately on arrival.

== Forces at Your Command

You control five major force types: planets, ships, starbases, armies, and ground batteries.

=== Planets
Planets generate the production that pays for fleets, defenses, and expansion.
* **Potential Production**: The ceiling a planet can reach at full efficiency. Ranges from 10 to 150 across the galaxy.
* **Present Production**: What the planet can deliver right now. Grows toward Potential over time.

Taxes convert Present Production into spendable build points each year. See <<Economy and Taxes>> for the full tax rate, growth, and starbase economics model.

=== Ships
Ships operate in fleets. A fleet always moves at the speed of its slowest member. A fleet can hold from one to **3,000 ships** of mixed types.

[%autowidth.spread,options="header"]
|===
| Ship Type | Cost | Speed | Size | AS | DS | Tactical Role
| **Destroyer** | 5 | 6 | S | 1 | 1 | Fast, cheap screen. Escapes heavy fleets. Good for scouting.
| **Cruiser** | 15 | 5 | M | 3 | 3 | Balanced fighter. About 3x the power of a destroyer.
| **Battleship** | 45 | 4 | L | 9 | 10 | Heavy firepower anchor. About 3x the power of a cruiser. Slow but durable.
| **Scout** | 15 | 6 | S | 0 | 1 | Stealthy spy. A lone scout is hardest to detect.
| **Transport** | 5 | 5 | M | 0 | 1 | Unarmed. Carries one army. Essential for conquest.
| **ETAC** | 20 | 3 | L | 0 | 2 | Colony ship. Reusable -- survives colonization.
|===

=== Starbases
Large space fortresses (Cost: 50, AS: 10, DS: 12) that play dual roles:

1.  **In Orbit**: They protect the planet and provide a defensive combat bonus. A planet with a starbase can withstand higher taxes without stalling growth. It also allows the planet to spend up to **5x** its current production on a single build (if points are accumulated). See <<Economy and Taxes>> for details.
2.  **In Deep Space**: They act as surveillance platforms with slightly more firepower than a battleship. They move very slowly (1 sector/year).

NOTE: Starbases must be commissioned into a fleet before they can be moved or provide benefits. An uncommissioned starbase in stardock has no effect.

=== Ground Forces
*   **Armies** (Cost: 2, AS: 1, DS: 1): Defend planets from invasion and capture enemy worlds. The only way to take a planet is to land armies and defeat the defenders. Each troop transport carries one army.
*   **Ground Batteries** (Cost: 20, AS: 9, DS: 2): Immobile land-based cannons. They offer massive firepower per cost (roughly equal to a battleship at less than half the price). During invasion, batteries must be destroyed before transports can land. During blitz, surviving batteries fire directly on descending transports. If a planet is captured via blitz, surviving batteries transfer intact to the new owner.

== Economy and Taxes

Your empire runs on production. Every owned planet generates tax revenue each maintenance cycle, and that revenue pays for ships, defenses, and expansion. Managing the tension between short-term revenue and long-term growth is one of the deepest strategic challenges in the game.

=== Key Terms

*   **Potential Production**: The maximum productive capacity a planet can reach.
*   **Present Production**: What the planet can deliver right now. Grows toward Potential over time.
*   **Total Available Points**: Your empire's spendable tax revenue this turn (sum across all planets).
*   **Stored Production Points**: Accumulated unspent points on a specific planet, available for future builds.

=== Tax Revenue

The tax rate is empire-wide -- you set one rate for all your planets. Revenue per planet per year is:

    revenue = floor(present_production * tax_rate / 100)

Your empire's Total Available Points is the sum of this across all owned planets.

=== Growth Toward Potential

Each maintenance turn, every owned planet grows its Present Production toward its Potential Production. The growth rate depends on your tax rate:

*   **Lower taxes produce faster growth.** A planet at 30% tax develops far faster than one at 60%.
*   **Growth slows naturally** as a planet approaches its Potential.
*   **Minimum growth**: A planet below its Potential always grows by at least 1 point per year, even at high taxes.

=== The 65% Tax Threshold

WARNING: Setting taxes above **65%** can directly *reduce* Present Production on your planets, not just slow growth.

*   Below 65%: Growth is positive. Lower is faster.
*   Above 65%: A penalty kicks in that actively damages Present Production.
*   **With a starbase**: The safe threshold rises to approximately **70%**, but there is no guarantee. Starbase worlds tolerate higher taxes better, but pushing past 70% still risks damage.

The recommended early-game rate is around 50-65%. Drop taxes on new colonies to accelerate their development.

=== Starbase Economic Effects

A commissioned starbase in orbit provides two major economic benefits:

1.  **Growth bonus**: The planet's yearly production growth is boosted by **+50%** over the base rate. Underdeveloped planets with starbases catch up significantly faster.
2.  **Build capacity multiplier**: The planet can spend up to **5x** its Present Production on builds in a single turn, drawing from Stored Production Points. Without a starbase, the planet can only spend up to 1x its Present Production per turn.

NOTE: These bonuses require an active, commissioned starbase in orbit -- not an uncommissioned starbase sitting in stardock.

=== Stored Production Points

Tax revenue that you do not spend accumulates as Stored Production Points on each planet. This reserve is what allows starbase worlds to execute large builds (up to 5x Present Production) in a single turn.

=== Newly Colonized Planets

A freshly colonized planet starts with Present Production far below its Potential. Growth depends heavily on the tax rate you set. Keep taxes low on new colonies to develop them quickly.

=== Conquered Planets

When you capture an enemy planet by invasion or blitz, the factories need approximately **two turns** before they are fully converted and begin producing tax revenue for your empire. Plan your logistics around this delay.

== Combat Mechanics

While battle reports are simple summaries, the engine uses a sophisticated system behind the curtain. The combat system relies on a deterministic, simultaneous-resolution model rather than opaque random number generation or arbitrary ship-vs-ship duels.

=== The Rules of Battle
*   **Simultaneous Resolution**: There is no "first strike" advantage. The total **Attack Strength (AS)** of each fleet is calculated and inflicted upon the enemy at the same exact moment. Rounds repeat until one side is destroyed, disengages, or only one hostile force remains.
*   **Nominal -> Crippled -> Destroyed**: Damage reduces ships from "Nominal" to "Crippled" status before destroying them. All nominal ships must be crippled before any crippled ship is destroyed. Surviving crippled ships are repaired automatically after battle.
*   **Combat Line Screening**: Hits target the combat line first -- destroyers, cruisers, battleships, and starbases. **Scouts, transports, and ETACs are protected** while any combat-line ships remain. This means your non-combat ships survive as long as your warships hold the line.
*   **Combined Arms Bonus**: Mixed fleets containing destroyers, cruisers, *and* battleships receive a tactical effectiveness bonus compared to single-type fleets. Always mix your fleet composition when possible.
*   **Starbase Defense Bonus**: A defending starbase at its own world provides an additional combat bonus to the defender. Starbases contribute to orbital defense only at the planet they orbit.
*   **Tie-Breakers**: In a draw, the defender (or the fleet holding position) wins.

=== Fleet Limits

A fleet can contain as few as one ship and as many as **3,000 ships** of mixed types. A fleet always moves at the speed of its slowest member.

=== Rules of Engagement (ROE)

You assign an ROE level (0-10) to control when your fleet voluntarily engages hostile forces. ROE is a deterministic commitment rule based on force ratios, not random chance.

[%autowidth.spread,options="header"]
|===
| ROE | Force Requirement | Behavior
| 0 | Never engage | **Pacifist**: Flee from all hostile fleets.
| 1 | Enemy defenseless | **Opportunist**: Engage only if the enemy has no combat capability.
| 2 | 4:1 or better | **Very Cautious**: Engage only with overwhelming advantage.
| 3 | 3:1 or better | **Cautious**: Engage only with strong advantage.
| 4 | 2:1 or better | **Favorable**: Engage with clear superiority.
| 5 | 3:2 or better | **Confident**: Engage with moderate advantage.
| 6 | 1:1 or better | **Balanced**: Engage equal or inferior forces.
| 7 | Even if outgunned 3:2 | **Bold**: Accept moderate disadvantage.
| 8 | Even if outgunned 2:1 | **Aggressive**: Accept significant disadvantage.
| 9 | Even if outgunned 3:1 | **Reckless**: Accept severe disadvantage.
| 10 | Always | **Suicidal**: Attack regardless of the odds.
|===

Non-combat fleets (scouts, transports, ETACs only) are treated as ROE 0 automatically.

=== Withdrawal and Retreat

IMPORTANT: Low ROE does not guarantee safety.

*   A fleet that refuses engagement or breaks off mid-battle does **not** escape cleanly. It suffers a **withdrawal exchange** -- the enemy gets to fire on your fleet as it retreats, and your fleet fires back at reduced effectiveness.
*   Only after absorbing that exchange does the fleet retreat and abort its current mission.
*   After each round of combat, surviving fleets re-check their ROE. If the post-loss ratio no longer meets their threshold, they attempt to disengage (and suffer the withdrawal exchange).

=== Planetary Combat

When fleets attack planets (bombardment, invasion, blitz), different rules apply:

*   **Planetary return fire**: Ground batteries are the primary anti-orbital weapon. Armies also contribute partially to return fire.
*   **Bombardment targeting priority**: Stardock contents (docked ships) > ground batteries > armies > stored goods > factories/development.
*   **Only combat ships contribute**: Destroyers, cruisers, and battleships contribute bombardment firepower. Scouts, transports, and ETACs do not.

See <<Missions and Orders>> for the detailed mechanics of bombardment, invasion, and blitz missions.

== Missions and Orders

A fleet always has exactly one standing order. If you issue a new order before maintenance, it replaces whatever the fleet was doing. Fleet missions fall into three categories that govern how long they stay active:

*   *One-shot*: Travel, perform the action, then revert to Hold Position. You must issue new orders afterward.
*   *Persistent*: Remain armed until you replace them or game rules invalidate them (e.g., the host fleet for a Join is destroyed).
*   *Hostile (delayed-resolution)*: Travel to the target world and wait. The assault executes on the *next* maintenance tick after the fleet is in orbit -- not immediately upon arrival. Plan accordingly.

[%autowidth.spread,options="header"]
|===
| No | Mission | Type | Description
| 0 | None | Persistent | Hold position at current location.
| 1 | Move Fleet | One-shot | Travel to a specified sector, then revert to Hold.
| 2 | Seek Home | One-shot | Return to nearest owned planet; retargets if that planet is captured en route.
| 3 | Patrol | Persistent | Move within sector deep space to intercept enemies.
| 4 | Guard Starbase | Persistent | Escort a starbase; fight alongside it.
| 5 | Blockade | Persistent | Prevent access to a planet. Stops enemy launches and landings.
| 6 | Bombard | Hostile | Damage factories, batteries, armies, stardock contents, and production.
| 7 | Invade | Hostile | Suppress batteries, bombard, then land armies. Requires loaded transports.
| 8 | Blitz | Hostile | Drop armies immediately, dodging batteries. High army risk.
| 9 | View | One-shot | Long-range scan of owner and production from system edge.
| 10 | Scout Sector | One-shot | Passive stealth surveillance of sector deep space (requires Scout).
| 11 | Scout System | One-shot | Active spy mission into a solar system (requires Scout).
| 12 | Colonize | One-shot | Terraform and claim unowned planet (requires ETAC). ETAC survives for reuse.
| 13 | Join Fleet | Persistent | Chase and merge with target fleet. Abandons if host is destroyed.
| 14 | Rendezvous | Persistent | Meet other fleets at sector. Lowest fleet ID becomes host.
| 15 | Salvage | One-shot | Scrap ships at planet for ~50% of build cost.
|===

=== Mission Details

==== One-Shot Missions

**Mission 1: Move to Sector**
*   A simple transit order.
*   Once the fleet arrives at the destination sector, it stops and its standing order automatically reverts to **Mission 0 (Hold Position)**. You must issue new orders if you want it to do anything else.
*   A fleet moves at the speed of its slowest ship.

**Mission 2: Seek Home**
*   The fleet travels to the nearest planet you own.
*   If that planet is captured while the fleet is en route, the fleet automatically redirects to the *next* nearest friendly planet.
*   On arrival, it reverts to **Hold Position**.

**Mission 9: View a World**
*   A safe, long-range scan. The fleet approaches the edge of the target system, scans for owner and production data, and immediately backs off into deep space.
*   The fleet reverts to **Hold Position** in deep space after reporting.

**Missions 10 & 11: Scout Sector / Scout System**
*   Both require at least one Scout ship. A fleet consisting of a single Scout is the least likely to be detected.
*   **Scout Sector (10)**: Passive, stealthy patrol. Unlike Mission 3, this fleet **will not engage** enemies. It relies on stealth to observe traffic without being seen.
*   **Scout System (11)**: An active spy run. The Scout penetrates the system to report:
    1.  Ground batteries and armies.
    2.  Current production and stardock contents.
    3.  Orbiting fleets.
*   Both revert to **Hold Position** after reporting.

**Mission 12: Colonize a World**
*   Requires at least one ETAC (Environmental Transformation And Colonization ship).
*   If the planet is unowned, it is terraformed and claimed. The new colony starts with one garrison army and very low current production, which grows faster under low taxes.
*   The ETAC is **not consumed** -- it survives and can colonize additional planets.
*   If the planet is *already* owned, the ETAC aborts, reports the owner's identity and production potential, and waits for new orders.

**Mission 15: Salvage**
*   The fleet travels to the specified planet and scraps its ships for approximately **50%** of the original build cost, returned as stored production points on that planet.
*   Reverts to **Hold Position** after scrapping (or fails if conditions are not met).

==== Persistent Standing Missions

**Mission 0: Hold Position**
*   The default idle state. The fleet stays at its current location and takes defensive action based on ROE if hostile fleets approach.

**Mission 3: Patrol a Sector**
*   Fleets move within the sector's deep space to intercept enemies passing through.
*   *Reporting*: If the patrol spots anything, it sends a report.
*   *Combat*: Patrols engage based on your ROE settings.
*   Remains active until you assign a new mission.

**Mission 4: Guard Starbase**
*   The fleet escorts the starbase and fights alongside it.
*   *Behavior*: If your fleet has a high ROE, it may break formation to chase enemy fleets that enter the system, leaving the starbase temporarily vulnerable.
*   Remains active until you assign a new mission.

**Mission 5: Guard/Blockade a World**
*   *Strategy of Denial*: A blockade stops enemy fleets from using the planet. It intercepts ships launching from Stardock and paralyzes the enemy's ability to deploy forces from that world.
*   Remains active until you assign a new mission.

**Missions 13 & 14: Fleet Coordination**
*   **Mission 13 (Join Fleet)**: The fleet chases a specific host fleet and merges with it when they meet. If the host fleet is destroyed before they meet, the joining fleet **abandons** the mission.
*   **Mission 14 (Rendezvous)**: Multiple fleets meet at a sector. The fleet with the **lowest Fleet ID** becomes the host/leader of the new combined fleet. The rendezvous point remains active so additional fleets can keep merging there.

==== Hostile (Delayed-Resolution) Missions

IMPORTANT: Hostile missions require the fleet to be **in orbit at the start of maintenance** to execute. A fleet that arrives at the target world this turn will carry out its assault **next turn**. This one-turn delay is a critical tactical consideration -- defend accordingly.

**Mission 6: Bombard a World**
*   Only destroyers, cruisers, and battleships contribute bombardment firepower. Scouts, transports, and ETACs do not contribute.
*   *Targeting priority*: Bombardment hits stardock contents first, then ground batteries, then armies, then stored goods, then factories and development.
*   *Planetary return fire*: Ground batteries and armies fire back at the bombarding fleet.
*   *Use Case*: Soften up a world before invasion, or deny resources to an enemy by destroying production and stardock contents.

**Mission 7: Invade a World**
*   A three-stage deliberate assault:
    1.  **Orbital suppression**: Combat ships exchange fire with ground batteries. Transports **cannot land** until all batteries are destroyed. Even if suppression fails to clear the batteries this turn, it still damages the planet.
    2.  **Softening fire**: Once batteries are gone, surviving combat ships inflict bombardment-style damage on armies and industry.
    3.  **Landing battle**: Transports land armies to fight survivors. Ground combat resolves simultaneously; defender wins ties.
*   *Capture*: Success requires destroying all defending armies. Your surviving armies become the new garrison. Destroyed batteries remain destroyed.
*   *Result*: High success rate, but damages the planet's factories significantly. Conquered planets need approximately two turns before factories are fully converted and start producing tax revenue for you.

**Mission 8: Blitz a World**
*   Transports drop armies immediately in a fast assault that bypasses the full orbital suppression sequence.
*   Escorting combat ships provide brief cover fire against batteries, but surviving batteries **fire directly on descending transports** during the landing, causing heavy transport losses.
*   Landed armies fight defending armies immediately. Defender receives a defensive bonus.
*   *Capture*: If you take the planet, surviving ground batteries **transfer intact** to your control.
*   *Result*: Low planetary damage (factories survive), but **high risk** to your armies and transports. A **2:1 army advantage** or better is recommended.
*   *When to Blitz*: When the planet has few or no batteries and you want to preserve its industry, or when speed matters more than casualties.

== Interface and Commands

The game uses four primary menus that map to the modern interface:

=== Main Menu
*   **General Command ("G")**: Autopilot, Diplomacy, Reports.
*   **Planet Command ("P")**: Economy and Production.
*   **Fleet Command ("F")**: Ship movement and missions.
*   **Information Database ("I")**: Review known planet data.
*   **View Starmap ("V")**: Graphic map of the galaxy.

=== General Command
*   **Autopilot ("A")**: Computer manages defenses if you miss turns.
*   **Diplomacy ("E")**: Declare players Neutral or Enemy.
    *   *Neutral*: Fleets will not attack unless provoked.
    *   *Enemy*: Fleets will attack on sight based on ROE.
*   **Messages ("C")**: Send messages to other empires.

=== Planet Command
*   **Tax ("T")**: Set empire-wide tax rate.
*   **Scorch Earth ("S")**: Destroy factories to deny them to an invader.
*   **Build ("B")**: Spend production points on ships, defenses, or starbases.
*   **Commission ("C")**: Assign new ships from Stardock into active fleets.
*   **Load/Unload ("L"/"U")**: Move armies between surface and transports.

=== Fleet Command
*   **Mission ("O")**: Assign missions 0-15.
*   **ROE ("C")**: Change rules of engagement.
*   **Merge ("M")**: Combine fleets in the same sector.
*   **Transfer ("T")**: Move ships between fleets.

=== Building and Commissioning Rules

Each planet has a **10-slot build queue**. All builds complete in **one maintenance turn**.

*   **Ships & Starbases**: Upon completion, they move to **Stardock**, a holding area on the planet. They sit idle and vulnerable there until you **Commission** them into an active fleet. Uncommissioned ships in stardock can be destroyed by enemy bombardment.
*   **Armies & Batteries**: Deploy directly to the planet surface immediately upon completion. They do **not** pass through stardock and do not need commissioning.

**Build capacity**: A planet without a starbase can spend up to its Present Production in a single turn. A planet with an orbiting starbase can spend up to **5x** its Present Production, drawing from Stored Production Points (see <<Economy and Taxes>>).

WARNING: Troop Transports are built empty. You must manually **Load ("L")** armies onto them before sending them into battle.

WARNING: Stardock contents are a prime target for enemy bombardment. Commission your ships promptly or risk losing them before they ever see combat.

== Strategy

=== Early Game (The Land Grab)
*   **Colonize Aggressively**: ETACs are your most valuable early unit. Grab every raw planet you can find.
*   **Balance Taxes**: High taxes (100%) give you cash now but stop growth. Stay at or below 65% to avoid damaging production. Drop taxes on new colonies.
*   **Scout**: Send single destroyers to find your neighbors before they find you.

=== Mid-Game (Consolidation)
*   **Build Starbases**: Place them on your best worlds to boost defense and production capacity.
*   **Intel is Key**: Never attack blindly. Use Scout ships (Mission 11) to count enemy batteries.
*   **The "Get Tough" Phase**: Once raw planets are gone, the only way to grow is war.

=== Late Game (Total War)
*   **Combined Arms**: Don't just build battleships. Mix destroyers, cruisers, and battleships to trigger the combined arms bonus and soak up damage across hull types.
*   **Denial**: If you can't hold a planet, **Scorch Earth**. If you can't take a planet, **Blockade** or **Bombard** it to uselessness.
*   **Diplomacy**: In a 25-player galaxy, you cannot fight everyone at once. Form alliances, even if they are temporary.

== Historical Context

**The BBS Era (1990-1992)**
Esterian Conquest was created by Bentley C. Griffith between 1990 and 1992 as a "door game" for Bulletin Board Systems. In an era before the World Wide Web, players dialed into servers over phone lines to play strategy games against dozens of strangers. Everything ran through rigid 80x25 text terminals -- every menu, every star map, every battle report squeezed into that tiny viewport. EC stood alongside classics like *TradeWars 2022* and *Solar Realms Elite*, but was distinguished by its depth, its hands-off design, and the sheer scale of its campaigns.

**What Made It Special**
Most multiplayer games of the era demanded constant attention. Esterian Conquest was different. You checked in once a day, submitted your orders, and went about your life. Overnight, the engine processed every empire simultaneously -- fleets moved, economies grew, battles resolved, and alliances were tested. When you logged in the next day, a stack of reports was waiting. Campaigns ran for months, and the stories they produced -- surprise invasions, desperate blockades, betrayals at the worst possible moment -- were the kind that stuck with players for years.

**The Rust Port (2026)**
This version is a faithful preservation of the original game, rebuilt from the ground up in Rust. Every rule, every formula, every mechanic has been recovered from the original binaries and validated for compatibility. If you played EC on a BBS in the 1990s, this is the same game. If you are discovering it for the first time, you are playing the real thing -- not a clone, not an approximation.

The immediate goal is a drop-in replacement that runs natively on modern systems without emulation, while preserving the classic experience for BBS sysops and Telnet players.

**Nostrian Conquest**
Looking further ahead, the project aims to free the game from centralized hosts entirely. Under the working title **Nostrian Conquest**, the next evolution will use the Nostr protocol as a transport layer -- players submit encrypted turn orders using cryptographic keys, and results are broadcast back through relays. No central server to shut down. No single point of failure. A serverless galaxy where the fog of war is enforced by cryptography. Freed from the shackles of the 80x25 Telnet screen, Nostrian Conquest will feature a full-screen modern ANSI/UTF-8 terminal interface worthy of the game's strategic depth.
